The Four Freedoms and What They Mean for Your Organization

April 10, 2026 Matthew Tift

Richard Stallman launched the free software movement in 1983 with an idea that sounded radical at the time: software users deserve freedom. Not just the freedom to run a program, but the freedom to understand it, share it, and improve it. Forty years later, those ideas power much of the internet, and they’re more relevant to nonprofits than most people realize.

The Free Software Foundation defines four freedoms. Here’s what each one looks like when you’re running a community organization instead of a tech company.

Freedom 0: Use the software for any purpose

This sounds obvious, but proprietary software licenses often restrict how you can use what you paid for. Some licenses limit the number of users. Others prohibit certain uses or require separate licenses for different departments. Free software doesn’t care who you are, how big your organization is, or what you do with it. Install it on one computer or fifty. Use it for fundraising, advocacy, education, or mutual aid. No one checks.

For a nonprofit that grows from three staff to thirty, this matters. Your software scales with you at no additional cost.

Freedom 1: Study how the software works

You probably won’t read the source code of LibreOffice or CiviCRM. Most people don’t. But the fact that you can means something. It means there are no hidden features collecting your data. It means security researchers can audit the code and find vulnerabilities before they cause harm. It means the software does what it says it does, and anyone can verify that.

When a proprietary company tells you their software is secure, you have to take their word for it. With free software, you don’t.

Freedom 2: Share copies with others

If your nonprofit finds a tool that works, you can share it with partner organizations, coalition members, or the group down the street doing similar work. You can install it for them. You can help them set it up. No licensing fees, no seat limits, no legal concerns.

This changes the math for entire networks of organizations. One group figures out how to use CiviCRM for donor management, and every allied group benefits. The knowledge and the software both spread freely.

Freedom 3: Modify the software and share your improvements

This is where free software gets interesting for organizations with needs that off-the-shelf tools don’t meet. If your nonprofit needs a feature that doesn’t exist, you (or someone you hire) can build it and contribute it back to the project. Other organizations with the same need benefit from your work, and you benefit from theirs.

The Drupal community, which builds the content management system that runs sites for the ACLU and Doctors Without Borders, works this way. Thousands of organizations contribute improvements that go back into the shared pool. A food shelf in Minneapolis and a human rights organization in Geneva both benefit from code that neither of them wrote alone.

Why this matters now

Software shapes how organizations operate. It determines what data you collect, how you communicate, and who has access to your work. Choosing software that respects your freedom isn’t a technical decision. It’s a decision about values, independence, and the kind of infrastructure you want your mission to depend on.
