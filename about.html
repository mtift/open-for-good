About

Why this site exists

Nonprofits and community organizations do some of the most important work in the world, and they do it with the least resources. When a food shelf spends $3,000 a year on software subscriptions, that’s $3,000 that doesn’t feed anyone. When a community arts program pays for Adobe licenses, that’s money that doesn’t go toward supplies or instructors. The math is simple, and it bothers me.

Free and open source software can change that equation. Tools like LibreOffice, CiviCRM, Drupal, and dozens of others can replace expensive proprietary software without sacrificing quality. These aren’t toy projects or half-baked experiments. They’re mature tools used by governments, universities, hospitals, and organizations around the world.

But most nonprofits don’t know these options exist. Or they’ve heard of “open source” and assume it’s only for programmers. This site exists to close that gap: to show mission-driven organizations that they can run their operations on software that respects both their budget and their values.

Why free software matters

The word “free” in free software doesn’t mean “no cost,” although most free software costs nothing to use. It means freedom. The Free Software Foundation defines four freedoms:

  1. The freedom to run the software for any purpose.
  2. The freedom to study how it works and change it.
  3. The freedom to share copies with others.
  4. The freedom to share your modifications.

These freedoms protect you. They mean no vendor can lock you into a contract, raise prices without warning, or shut down their product and leave you scrambling. They mean you own your data and your workflow. For nonprofits, which often depend on tools for years or decades, that stability matters.

There’s also a values question. Nonprofits exist to serve the common good. Free software exists to serve the common good. The alignment isn’t a coincidence. When software is built by communities rather than corporations, the incentives are different. No one is trying to maximize your engagement or extract your data. The software exists because people decided it should exist, and they built it together.

Who I am

My name is Matthew Tift. I’ve spent over a decade building websites with Drupal, one of the largest free software projects in the world. I work as a Lead Engineer at Lullabot, where I help organizations build and maintain their web presence using open source tools.

I care about this stuff because I’ve seen what free software makes possible. I’ve watched nonprofits save thousands of dollars by switching away from proprietary tools. I’ve seen community groups build websites they can maintain themselves, without ongoing costs. And I’ve been part of a free software community, the Drupal community, that proves people can build something useful and share it with the world.

I built this site with Publii, a free desktop application for creating static websites, and I host it on GitHub Pages at no cost. The whole thing, from the CMS to the hosting to the theme, is free. That felt like the right way to do it.
