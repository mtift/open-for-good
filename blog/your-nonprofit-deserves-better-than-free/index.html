Your Nonprofit Deserves Better Than "Free"

April 10, 2026 Matthew Tift

When someone offers you something for free, it’s worth asking what they get out of it.

Gmail costs nothing. Google Docs costs nothing. Facebook and Instagram cost nothing. These tools work well, and nonprofits use them everywhere. But they aren’t free in any meaningful sense. You pay with your data, your donors’ data, and your community’s attention. Google reads your email to serve ads. Facebook decides who sees your posts and charges you to reach the people who already follow you. These companies built their business models around extracting value from the people who use their products.

For a nonprofit, this should feel uncomfortable. Organizations that exist to serve their communities shouldn’t have to hand over sensitive information to use basic tools. A domestic violence shelter using Gmail is trusting Google with the email addresses of survivors. A food shelf using a “free” donor management platform is giving a company access to its supporters’ names and giving history. Maybe nothing bad happens with that data. But the point is that you don’t control what happens with it, and you can’t.

The other kind of free

Free and open source software offers a different deal. When the Free Software Foundation talks about “free software,” they mean software that respects your freedom. You can use it however you want. You can look at the code and see what it does. You can share it. You can change it. No one tracks you, locks you in, or mines your data.

This isn’t idealism. It’s practical. When your donor management system runs on CiviCRM, the data stays on your server. When your team writes documents in LibreOffice, nobody analyzes those documents to sell advertising. When your website runs on Drupal or Publii, you own every piece of it.

The cost question

Yes, free and open source software saves money. That matters for organizations running on tight budgets. But the money question can distract from the more important one: who controls your tools?

Proprietary software companies can change their pricing whenever they want. They can discontinue products. They can alter terms of service. If your organization builds its operations around a tool that a company controls, you’re dependent on that company’s decisions. Free software removes that dependency. The code belongs to everyone. If one group stops maintaining it, another can pick it up. Your investment in learning and configuring the tool doesn’t evaporate because a company pivoted to a new market.

Getting started

If this resonates but feels overwhelming, check out the Getting Started page. The short version: pick one tool, try it, and go from there. You don’t need to change everything at once. You just need to start asking better questions about the tools you use and who benefits from your answers.
