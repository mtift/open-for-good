Getting Started

Switching to free software can feel like a big move, especially if your organization has been using the same tools for years. The good news: you don’t have to do it all at once. In fact, you shouldn’t. Start with one tool, get comfortable, and go from there.

Pick the easiest win first

Look at what your organization pays for and ask which subscription hurts the most. For a lot of nonprofits, that’s the office suite. Microsoft 365 subscriptions for a small team can cost hundreds of dollars a year. Installing LibreOffice takes about ten minutes, it opens all your existing files, and your team can start using it the same day.

That first switch matters more for morale than for money. Once people see that free software works, the next conversation gets easier.

Don’t migrate everything at once

A common mistake is trying to replace every tool in one weekend. That leads to frustration and, often, giving up entirely. Instead, pick one thing at a time. Use LibreOffice for a month before thinking about email. Get comfortable with your new website before tackling donor management. Each change needs time to settle before you add another.

Ask whether you need the feature or just the habit

When people resist switching tools, the reason is often habit rather than need. “But we need Track Changes” comes up a lot with word processors. LibreOffice has Track Changes. “But we need pivot tables.” LibreOffice has pivot tables. Go through the features your team uses and check whether the free alternative covers them. In most cases, it does. The interface might look different, but the capability is there.

Sometimes the answer is honest: “We use a feature that doesn’t exist in the free version.” That’s fine. Keep using the paid tool for that task and switch the others. This isn’t all or nothing.

Find your community

One of the best parts of free software is that people who build it and use it want to help. Every tool listed in the Software Guide has forums, documentation, or chat channels where you can ask questions. The people answering are often volunteers who care about the project and enjoy helping newcomers.

If you’re in the Twin Cities, there are local groups that meet to talk about free software, including the Twin Cities Drupal community. Look for user groups in your area. Showing up to a meetup and saying “I run a food shelf and I’m trying to figure out CiviCRM” will get you more help than you expect.

Consider what you’re giving up when software is “free”

When a company offers you software at no cost, it’s worth asking why. Gmail is free because Google reads your email to sell advertising. Facebook is free because it sells your attention and your data. The business model depends on you being the product.

Free and open source software works differently. It’s free because the people who build it believe software should respect your freedom. The code is open, the community maintains it, and no one is selling your data behind the scenes. That distinction matters for nonprofits that hold sensitive information about donors, clients, or community members.

You don’t need to be technical

This comes up a lot, and it stops people before they start. Installing LibreOffice or Thunderbird requires the same skill as installing any other program: download it, click install, open it. Tools like Publii have a visual interface where you type your content, pick a theme, and click a button to publish your website. CiviCRM and Drupal take more effort to set up, but you can find consultants and community members who will help, often at rates far below what the proprietary alternatives cost for licensing alone.

The goal isn’t to become a developer. The goal is to run your organization on tools that respect your freedom and your budget, so you can focus on the work that matters.
