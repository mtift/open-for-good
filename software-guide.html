Software Guide

Most nonprofits rely on the same core functions: tracking donors, managing money, communicating with people, creating documents, and maintaining a website. For each of these, there’s free and open source software that does the job well. Some of these tools have been around for decades, maintained by communities of developers and users who believe software should serve everyone.

This guide organizes tools by what you need to get done, not by technical category. If something here catches your eye, try it. Most of these tools can be installed in minutes, and none of them will ask for a credit card.

Donor and Contact Management

CiviCRM handles donor tracking, membership management, event registration, email campaigns, and case management. It was built for nonprofits and civic organizations, and thousands of groups around the world use it. CiviCRM integrates with content management systems like Drupal and WordPress, so it can work alongside your website. There’s a learning curve, but the community offers documentation and support forums to help you through it.

Accounting and Finances

GnuCash does double-entry accounting for small organizations. It tracks income, expenses, and accounts, and it can generate the reports your board or funders expect to see. It runs on Windows, Mac, and Linux. If you’ve used QuickBooks, GnuCash will feel familiar, though the interface takes some getting used to.

Office Suite

LibreOffice replaces Microsoft Office. It includes a word processor, spreadsheet, presentation tool, and more. It reads and writes Microsoft file formats, so you can share documents with people who use Word or Excel without compatibility headaches. LibreOffice has been around in one form or another since 2000, and it keeps getting better.

Email

Thunderbird is a free email client from the same organization that makes Firefox. It handles multiple email accounts, has a built-in calendar, and supports encryption if your work requires secure communication. For organizations that want control over their email without relying on Gmail or Outlook, Thunderbird is a solid choice.

Website

WordPress powers roughly 40% of all websites on the internet. It’s free, flexible, and has a massive community of developers creating themes and plugins. For nonprofits that need a website with a blog, event listings, or donation pages, WordPress can handle it. Just make sure you use the self-hosted version at wordpress.org, not the commercial service at wordpress.com.

Drupal is another free content management system, used by organizations like Doctors Without Borders, the ACLU, and the University of Minnesota. Drupal is powerful and accessible, and it has a strong commitment to values and principles that align well with mission-driven work. It takes more effort to set up than WordPress, but for organizations with complex needs, it’s worth considering.

Publii is what this site runs on. It’s a desktop application that generates a static website you can host for free on platforms like GitHub Pages. No server to maintain, no monthly hosting fees, no security patches to worry about. For a small organization that needs a simple, fast website, Publii is hard to beat.

Design and Graphics

GIMP is a free image editor that handles photo retouching, image composition, and graphic design. It does what Photoshop does, though the interface works differently. Inkscape handles vector graphics, which means logos, illustrations, and print-ready artwork. Between the two, most nonprofit design needs are covered.

Team Communication

Mattermost works like Slack but can be self-hosted, so your conversations stay on your own server. For organizations that handle sensitive information, this matters. Nextcloud goes further, offering file sharing, calendars, contacts, and collaborative document editing, all under your control. Think of it as a self-hosted alternative to Google Workspace.

Video Conferencing

Jitsi Meet offers free video calls with no account required. Share a link, and people can join from a browser. No downloads, no sign-ups. For a nonprofit that runs board meetings or volunteer check-ins over video, Jitsi removes the friction and the cost.

A Note on “Free”

The word “free” in free software refers to freedom, not just price. The Free Software Foundation defines four freedoms: the freedom to run the software for any purpose, to study how it works, to share it with others, and to modify it and share your modifications. These freedoms mean that no company can lock you in, raise prices, or shut down the service and leave you stranded. For nonprofits that depend on their tools to carry out their mission, that independence matters.
